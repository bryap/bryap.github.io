Do you feel a SPARK?: Studying the Spectrum of Physiological Awe, Resilience, and Knowing

Brya Patterson, Nicco Reggente, Matthew Lee, et al., Spring 2026, Institute for Advanced Consciousness Studies and University of Washington, MS Information Management (MSIM)

June 06, 2026 Brya K. Patterson

Executive Summary

According to the World Health Organization, over 30% of college students screen positive for a mental disorder being prevalent within the last 12 months (Auerbach et. al, 2018). This research study aims to gain more understanding and insight into correlations between awe and mental wellbeing to identify possible strategies to support mental health. It will specifically focus on the college student population because this is a common stage in life where many people face mental health challenges that could benefit from data-driven support.

  1. The Mission: In collaboration with the Institute for Advanced Consciousness Studies (IACS), our team developed and piloted a 67-item digital survey instrument to explore potential correlations between awe-related experiences—specifically the ability to voluntarily self-induce aesthetic chills—and consciousness constructs like openness, absorption, interoceptive awareness, and resilience.
  2. Impact: We successfully profiled the "Chills Controller," establishing that intentional physiological control over chills is a rare trait (found in 30.7% of respondents) and demonstrating that early-life nature exposure and high psychological absorption capacity are key predictive drivers of peak emotional states.
  3. Core Capabilities: Survey Design, Advanced Data Analytics, Machine Learning (Logistic Regression, K-Means Clustering, PCA), Stakeholder Strategy.

The Results: Quantifying Awe and Predictive Accuracy

Through campus canvassing and regional outreach, we collected 107 responses, retaining 101 high-quality data points after intensive screening to map out the unique psychological traits of individuals capable of physiological control.

Key analytical findings from our dataset included:

  • The Rarity of the Trait: Only 30.7% of data-screened participants (31 out of 101) reported the ability to intentionally induce chills without external stimuli, validating that voluntary control over this physiological response is uncommon.
  • Strong Baseline Body Awareness: Chills-capable individuals scored significantly higher (+0.30 difference) than non-inducers on listening to their body during stress, indicating a profound mind-body connection.
  • Openness as a Functional Predictor: In evaluating predictive modeling performance, Logistic Regression models utilizing the Openness to Experience construct achieved a higher average accuracy (68.5%) compared to models utilizing the Absorption construct (60%).
  • The Modeling Challenge: Due to the pilot sample scale (n=101), both predictive models faced high variability and struggled to cleanly isolate "Yes" instances, pointing to the clear necessity for a larger dataset in future iterations.
This preliminary framework provides concrete evidence that peak awe experiences are rooted in distinct, identifiable behavioral patterns rather than random distribution.

Permeable Psychological Profile

Individuals who voluntarily control chills possess a highly "permeable" psychology—exhibiting notably higher openness to experience, vibrant internal dream worlds, and environmental absorption.

Environmental Sensitivity

Early-life exposure to awe-inspiring natural settings (such as growing up near large bodies of water or nature) serves as a top-five historical predictor for adult chill induction.

Spiritual & Naturalist Resonance

Deep connection during meditation/prayer and belief in universal energies (e.g., chakras, kundalini) emerged as the absolute highest-weighted predictors for the SPARK trait.

The Methodology: Iterative Development and Campus Canvassing

To bridge the gap between abstract consciousness constructs and empirical data, the team conducted an extensive literature review covering awe, psychology, and aesthetic chills. In partnership with project sponsors at IACS, we executed an iterative design process to build a robust 67-item digital survey on Google Forms. After completing internal take/retake reliability checks across our team, we deployed the tool live—canvassing the University of Washington campus, tabling at university events, and distributing links through collegiate networks across Washington, Oregon, and California.

Advanced Analytics: Uncovering Awe Phenotypes

Data visualization and exploration were facilitated using a custom-built Power BI dashboard. From there, our primary analysis utilized a rigorous array of methodologies, including Logistic Regression, K-Means Clustering combined with Principal Component Analysis (PCA), Lasso, and Random Forest models to target four core research inquiries.

Demographic Clustering and Worldviews

Using the elbow method, we determined that 3 was the optimal number of clusters for classifying our participants. When isolating the chills-capable "Inducers," we discovered unique phenotypic splits. Notably, one distinct cluster consisted of older, highly educated individuals who could induce chills but leaned heavily toward an Atheist/Naturalist worldview. This contrasted sharply with younger clusters who favored traditional Religious/Theistic perspectives, proving that physiological awe transcends specific spiritual frameworks.

Stress Remediation and Living Backgrounds

Our analysis revealed a positive correlation (+0.14) between chill capability and physical stress signaling. Interestingly, background environment heavily influenced coping mechanisms: chills-capable respondents from rural backgrounds demonstrated a strong tendency to actively seek out awe experiences for relief (scoring 4.25/5.0), whereas highly urban participants reported the highest baseline resilience scores but were the least likely to utilize awe as a remediation tool.

Project Impact and Next Steps

The implications of this research stretch across emotional, mental, and social boundaries. Learning to identify and harness physiological responses like chills can fundamentally strengthen the mind-body connection, change the lens through which we view uncertainty, and enhance overall emotional resilience and creativity.

Moving forward, the roadmap for the SPARK study centers on three critical goals:

  • Scale Data Collection: Expanding our regional footprint across collegiate networks to build a larger sample size, directly improving machine learning model stability and predictive reliability.
  • Targeted Immersive Research: Aligning closer with IACS to use virtual reality and experiential technology to stimulate the specific "Openness-Absorption" patterns identified in this pilot.
  • Fostering a Community: Establishing an optional, dedicated mailing network for our identified "inducers" to directly assist with future technological and clinical studies.
As our project sponsors at IACS perfectly encapsulate: "Goosebumps are just the beginning."
